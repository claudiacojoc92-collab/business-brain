# M2.2 — Upload Connector (Capability Spec)

**Type:** Capability Specification (product · UX · architecture · engineering)
**Status:** APPROVED — governed by ADR-007 (LOCKED)
**Phase:** Capability Build (first capability after Foundation Phase / M2.1)
**Date:** 2 July 2026
**Authored as:** CTO first, engineer second.

> **Governing principle (frozen, ADR-007):** Business Brain learns from connected reality (`observed`) and declared intent (`declared`). It never asks founders to reconstruct what it can fetch. Reconstruction is permanently forbidden. **A different file extension does not change the principle.**

> **Unit of delivery (new, per Capability Build Phase):** the smallest *complete* capability, not the smallest slice. M2.2 ships when a founder can upload genuine business artifacts and Business Brain understands them under the same honesty guarantees as the Website Connector — end to end. One internal review gate is retained (see §14) at the single architectural-risk point: artifact classification.

---

## 0. The CTO challenge, stated first

You named the core problem correctly: **the first architectural problem is not parsing — it's distinguishing a genuine uploaded artifact from disguised reconstruction.** I want to sharpen it further, because the naive framing of that problem is unsolvable and will waste the milestone if we accept it.

**The naive framing:** "detect whether an uploaded file is reconstruction." This is unsolvable. A founder can paste their About page into a PDF, export it, and upload it — and at the *byte level* it is indistinguishable from a genuine one-page strategy memo. There is no classifier that reads content and reliably returns "this was retyped to bypass the rule." Intent is not recoverable from bytes. If we build M2.2 around a content-inspecting reconstruction-detector, we will build something that is either leaky (false negatives let reconstruction in) or insulting (false positives reject a founder's real document), and we will have spent the milestone on an unwinnable classifier.

**The reframing that IS solvable:** we do not need to detect reconstruction. We need to make reconstruction *pointless and structurally disfavored*, and be honest about provenance so that whatever gets uploaded is represented for exactly what it is. Three moves, developed in §4 and §5:

1. **Provenance, not policing.** An uploaded artifact is `observed` evidence *of a document the founder possesses* — not `observed` evidence of external reality the way a fetched website is. That is a real epistemic difference and the honesty model must encode it. We don't judge the founder's intent; we accurately record what the evidence *is*: "this is what your uploaded document says," never silently promoted to "this is what your business is."

2. **Remove the incentive.** Reconstruction happens when manual entry is the *easiest path to being understood*. M2.1 already made connected reality the easy path. The upload connector must be positioned as "give me documents you already have," never "tell me about your business in a file." If uploading a retyped About page is *more* effort than just connecting the website (which M2.1 already reads), the incentive to reconstruct collapses on its own.

3. **Duplicate-detection, not intent-detection.** The one case we *can* detect mechanically and honestly: an uploaded artifact whose content substantially overlaps something Business Brain already has as `observed` connected reality (the website it already read). That's not "is this reconstruction?" — it's "is this redundant with connected reality I already trust?" — which is a real, decidable question, and it catches the most common reconstruction vector (retyping the website) as a *side effect* of deduplication rather than as an accusation.

The spec below is built on this reframing. If we accept the naive framing instead, I'd recommend *not* building M2.2 as specified — so this reframing is the first thing to approve or reject.

## 1. Vision

The Upload Connector completes the "Connect Your World" promise for the large class of business reality that lives in **documents, not URLs.** A founder's positioning, offers, strategy, and brand frequently exist most truthfully in a pitch deck, a brand guide, or a strategy memo — artifacts no website fetch or OAuth can reach. Upload is not a fallback for when connectors fail; it is a first-class connector for a first-class category of evidence: the documents a business already produced.

The capability is complete when a founder can hand Business Brain the artifacts they already have and receive the same two-beat, fully-traceable understanding the Website Connector delivers — with every claim traced to a specific place in a specific document, honestly thin when a document is thin, never fabricated, and never allowing a retyped-webpage to masquerade as new reality.

**Strategic point:** uploads deepen the moat differently than the website does. The website is public — a competitor's fresh sync reproduces it. A founder's *private* strategy deck, brand guide, or internal handbook is not public and not re-syncable. Upload is where connected reality starts to include things only *this* founder could give Business Brain — which makes it, alongside declared intent, part of the defensible layer.

## 2. Founder experience

The experience mirrors the website magic moment's shape — fast grounded recognition, then deepening — adapted to documents.

**Beat 0 — the ask (positioning matters).** The invitation is *"Share documents you already have — a pitch deck, brand guide, strategy doc, an export from a tool you use."* It is explicitly *not* "write about your business" or "tell us more." The framing names real artifacts, because the framing is the first line of defense against reconstruction (§0, move 2). A founder who has nothing to upload is routed onward, not pressured to manufacture a document.

**Beat 1 — the read (fast, grounded).** On upload, Business Brain shows real progress — *"Reading your pitch deck… found 14 slides… reading your positioning…"* — then reflects back grounded observations from the document, each traceable to a specific location (slide, page, section). Same fast primary beat as the website.

**Beat 2 — the deepening (behind).** The inferred synthesis streams in behind Beat 1, now able to reason across *both* the uploaded documents and previously-connected reality (the website). This is a genuine upgrade over M2.1: the first time Business Brain fuses two sources, and the first time it can surface a *contradiction between what a founder publishes and what their internal docs say* — a high-value strategic signal.

**The honest edges.** A document that yields little produces a short honest reflection, not a padded one. A document that substantially duplicates already-connected reality is acknowledged as such — *"This looks like your website content, which I've already read — I'll skip the duplicate and keep anything new"* — never silently ingested as if it were fresh evidence. An unsupported or corrupt file fails honestly with a clear next step.

**The handoff.** As with M2.1, the reflection closes by inviting declared intent — the thing no document contains.

## 3. Supported artifacts

Scoped by *honest extractability* and *real-world frequency*, not by "what's technically parseable." Every supported type must yield location-specific provenance (a page, slide, or section a fragment can point at).

**Tier 1 — ship in M2.2 (high frequency, clean extraction, clear provenance):**
- **PDF** — the dominant business-artifact format: pitch decks, brand guides, strategy docs, sales decks, handbooks. Page-level provenance. (Text-based PDFs in scope; scanned/image-only PDFs → honest "I can't read this one" unless OCR is explicitly added later — OCR is OUT for M2.2.)
- **DOCX** — strategy memos, plans, proposals. Section/heading-level provenance.
- **Markdown / plain text** — but see §4: these are the *highest reconstruction risk* and get the strictest classification treatment. Supported, not banned — a real exported doc can be markdown — but they carry the heaviest provenance/honesty scrutiny.

**Tier 2 — explicitly deferred (each named so scope is unambiguous):**
- **PPTX** — real (meeting decks) but its own extraction surface; defer unless review pulls it into T1.
- **LinkedIn / Notion / Google Docs exports** — these arrive as ZIP or HTML/MD bundles; valuable (LinkedIn export is the *only* honest path to LinkedIn content, per the M2 blueprint) but each is a distinct unpack-and-normalize problem. Defer to a follow-up capability; the architecture must not preclude them.
- **ZIP archives** — the container for most exports; deferred with the exports.
- **Images / scanned PDFs (OCR)** — deferred; honest-fail in M2.2.
- **Spreadsheets (XLSX/CSV)** — a different evidence shape (tabular, not prose); deferred, may not belong in this connector at all.

**Challenge:** I'd resist the temptation to support "everything a founder might have" in M2.2. Each format is a permanent maintenance liability (M2 blueprint §12) and a distinct extraction/provenance problem. PDF + DOCX + text covers the large majority of genuine business artifacts with clean provenance. Breadth is deferred behind real demand, exactly as the connector catalog is.

## 4. Artifact classification — the core design problem

This is the section the whole capability turns on. Per §0, we **reframe from intent-detection (unsolvable) to provenance-typing + redundancy-detection (solvable).**

Every uploaded artifact is classified along two independent axes, neither of which requires guessing the founder's intent:

**Axis A — Provenance type (what IS this evidence, epistemically):**
- **`observed-artifact`** — a document the founder possesses. This is the honest default for a genuine upload. It is `observed`, but it is `observed` *of a document*, not of external reality. The honesty model (§5) never lets it silently claim more than "your document says X."
- **`redundant`** — content that substantially overlaps evidence already held as connected reality (primarily: the website already read). Detected mechanically by content similarity against existing `observed` fragments. Not an accusation of reconstruction — a deduplication fact. Redundant content contributes no new evidence; genuinely-new portions of the same file still can.

**Axis B — Provenance strength (how location-specific is the artifact):**
- A structured artifact (PDF deck, DOCX with headings) yields **strong** provenance — fragments point at specific slides/pages/sections.
- A flat text/markdown blob yields **weak** provenance — no internal structure to anchor to. Weak-provenance artifacts are *supported but flagged*: they get the same honesty gate, but the reflection is more conservative because the anchoring is coarser.

**The reconstruction question, answered honestly:** we do not classify "is this reconstruction?" We classify *provenance type* and *redundancy*, and we let the honesty model represent every artifact as exactly what it is. This catches the real harm — reconstruction masquerading as new reality — without an unwinnable intent-classifier:
- Retyped website → detected as `redundant` → contributes nothing new, honestly acknowledged. The most common reconstruction vector is neutralized as a *deduplication side effect*.
- A genuine strategy memo that happens to be a text file → `observed-artifact`, weak provenance → ingested honestly, reflected conservatively.
- A file whose *only* content is "My business is a coaching business" → ingested as an `observed-artifact` that says exactly that, with weak provenance, contributing one thin fragment — *and it gains the founder nothing over just saying it in conversation as declared intent.* The incentive to do this collapses (§0, move 2); we don't need to detect and reject it, because it isn't harmful when represented honestly and it isn't advantageous to the founder.

**The one thing we DO hard-gate:** an uploaded artifact may never be typed `observed` of *external reality* (e.g. "the market believes X," "customers say Y") on the strength of the document alone — because a document a founder uploaded is evidence of *the document*, not of the market. The Website Connector reads the founder's public claims; upload reads the founder's private documents; neither is evidence of the outside world's independent state. This is the honest boundary and it is structural (§5).

## 5. Honesty model

Extends the M2.1 three-layer structural gate (DB CHECK · domain smart-constructor · store append) — unchanged and inherited — with upload-specific invariants. **No honesty mechanism is loosened; upload adds constraints, never relaxes them.**

Inherited, unchanged:
- Evidence typed `observed | declared | inferred`; `observed` requires a source; `inferred` requires non-empty `derived_from` (canonical: `evidenceRefs`/`evidenceChain` of `{source, fragment}`) resolving to real fragments; resolution fails closed; the reflection renders only traceable lines; no fuzzy matching.

Upload-specific additions:
1. **Document-source provenance.** Every upload fragment carries `source: upload`, the **document identity** (filename + content hash), and a **location anchor** (page/slide/section/char-range) so provenance points *inside the document*, not just "this file." An upload `observed` fragment with no location anchor on a structured artifact is invalid — provenance must be as specific as the document's structure allows (the same ratchet as M2.1's block-precision).
2. **Epistemic ceiling on uploads.** An upload fragment may assert *"your document states X"* — it may **not** be the sole basis for an `inferred` claim about *external reality* (market, competitors, customer sentiment). Inferences fusing upload + website are allowed and encouraged; inferences that launder a founder's private document into a claim about the outside world are forbidden. Enforced at the recompute layer: the engine's marketContext-class outputs may not cite an upload-only evidence chain (mirrors M2.1's rule that marketContext may not cite a founder source).
3. **Redundancy is represented, not hidden.** A `redundant` classification is a first-class, visible state (§10) — the founder is told their upload duplicated known reality. Redundant fragments do not enter the Business Model as new evidence (no double-counting a fact because it appears on the website *and* in an uploaded copy of the website).
4. **Weak-provenance conservatism.** Flat-text artifacts (no internal anchors) resolve at whole-document or paragraph granularity only; the reflection must not imply location-specificity the artifact doesn't support. Honest coarseness over false precision.
5. **The extension-invariance rule (frozen restatement).** The honesty gate treats content by *what it is and where it's anchored*, never by file type. A `.pdf` and a `.txt` containing the same retyped webpage are both caught as `redundant`; a genuine memo in either format is both `observed-artifact`. Extension is metadata, never a provenance signal.

## 6. Upload architecture

Mirrors the connector contract and the connector→evidence→engine→reflection boundary from M2.1. **The Upload Connector implements the same standard connector contract** (M2 blueprint §4) — it is not a special-cased endpoint; it is a connector whose `sync()` reads an uploaded file instead of fetching a URL. This is what keeps the five-year plug-in architecture intact.

```
upload (file) → Upload Connector → evidence store → engine → reflection
                (authorize/capabilities/sync/normalize/produceEvidence/status/disconnect)
```

Contract, upload-specialized:
- `authorize()` — no-op (upload is unauthenticated, like website). Full contract implemented, thin where unauthenticated.
- `capabilities()` — `{ read: true, insights: false, publish: false }`; declares supported types.
- `sync(fileRef)` — accept the uploaded file; detect type; route to the right extractor; return raw extracted content + document structure. Bounded (size cap, page cap, timeout — same discipline as the fetcher's bounds).
- `normalize()` — per artifact type: extract text + structure (pages/slides/sections) + document metadata; classify per §4 (provenance type via redundancy check against existing `observed` fragments; provenance strength via structure presence).
- `produceEvidence()` — emit `observed-artifact` fragments with `source: upload`, document identity, and location anchors, through the unchanged honesty gate. Redundant content emits nothing new.
- `status()` — honest states (§10): reading / synced / partial / empty / redundant / unsupported / failed.
- `disconnect()` — delete this founder's upload evidence + the stored file.

**The connector's only output is evidence** (inviolable boundary, inherited). It does not call the engine, compute the model, or reach downstream. The engine consumes only evidence and never knows a file was uploaded vs fetched — it sees typed fragments with provenance, identically.

**Recompute is shared, not rebuilt.** Upload evidence flows into the *same* recompute path M2.1 built (store → frozen engine → fail-closed resolution → two-beat reflection). No engine change (frozen, `992666a6…`/`6dff794d…`). The only recompute addition is that resolution now spans multiple sources (website + upload) — which the existing per-source resolution already supports; upload is just another source label.

## 7. Evidence generation

Per artifact type, producing `observed-artifact` fragments with location anchors:
- **PDF** → text per page; fragment anchor = page number (+ optional block within page, reusing M2.1's block-level provenance approach where the PDF has extractable structure). Deck-style PDFs (one concept per slide) anchor naturally at page granularity.
- **DOCX** → text per heading-delimited section; anchor = section/heading path. Leverages the document's own outline for provenance.
- **Text/Markdown** → paragraph-level anchors where markdown structure exists (headings, lists); whole-document weak provenance where it's a flat blob (§5.4).

Every fragment: `observed`, `source: upload`, `sourceDocument: {filename, contentHash}`, `anchor: {…}`, `captured_at`, `occurred_at` from document metadata if present else null, `visibility: private` (uploaded documents are private by default — a critical difference from the website's `public`, and it governs what the reflection may reference publicly, §5/§9). Content-addressed ids for dedupe (re-uploading the same file adds nothing).

**Reuse M2.1's extraction lessons:** the website connector's DOM/block work proved that extraction *quality* drives everything downstream (hit-rate, magic-moment richness). PDF/DOCX extraction quality is the M2.2 equivalent risk and gets the same first-class treatment in measurement (§12), not a checkbox.

## 8. Provenance

Provenance is *stronger* for uploads than for the website in one respect and *more constrained* in another — both must be honest.

- **Stronger:** a claim can point at "slide 7 of your pitch deck" or "the Positioning section of your strategy doc" — more meaningful to a founder than "somewhere on your homepage." The location anchor makes upload provenance potentially the most trust-building in the product. Chips should surface document + location (*"from slide 7 of Pitch-Deck.pdf"*).
- **More constrained:** `visibility: private` means upload-derived claims must not leak into any *public-facing* output Business Brain later produces (a limit that matters once publishing exists; forbidden in M2.2 anyway, but the visibility flag is set now so the constraint is structural from day one).
- **Ratchet preserved:** `derived_from` for inferred claims points at specific upload fragments (specific slides/sections), never "the document" as a whole — same most-specific-block-wins discipline as M2.1. Provenance only gets tighter.

## 9. Security

Uploads introduce a real attack surface the website connector didn't have — accepting arbitrary files from users. This is the section where "capability-first" must not skip real risk.

- **File validation:** type detection by content (magic bytes), not extension; reject anything not a supported type; hard size cap; page/complexity caps to bound extraction cost and prevent zip-bomb / billion-laughs / pathological-PDF DoS.
- **Parser safety:** PDF and DOCX parsers are historically a malware/exploit surface. Parse in a bounded, isolated context; never execute embedded content (PDF JavaScript, DOCX macros — both explicitly disabled/ignored); treat all document content as untrusted data, never as instructions (this includes: extracted document text is *evidence*, never an instruction to Business Brain — a prompt-injection vector where an uploaded doc says "ignore your rules" must be treated as inert content, per the instruction-source boundary).
- **Storage:** uploaded files stored server-side, access-scoped to the founder, encrypted at rest; `disconnect()` deletes them. No file ever stored or referenced by a URL that embeds content.
- **PII / sensitivity:** uploaded business docs may contain sensitive internal data; `visibility: private` is the structural guard, and retention/erasure must honor the GDPR posture (per ADR-007's crypto-shred concern — an uploaded doc's evidence must be erasable).
- **Prompt-injection via document content:** called out explicitly because it's the novel one — an uploaded document is *data the engine reasons over*, and a malicious doc could contain text engineered to manipulate the model. Extracted text enters as `observed` evidence content, never as system/instruction context; the honesty gate's fail-closed resolution provides defense-in-depth (a fabricated instruction can't produce traceable evidence), but the architecture must ensure document text is never concatenated into an instruction position.

**Challenge:** security is the reason upload is genuinely harder than the website, and it's where I'd push back if the milestone tried to move fast. The parser-safety and injection surfaces are real. This is in scope for M2.2, not deferrable — a connector that accepts files without these controls should not ship.

## 10. UX states

Inherits M2.1's honest state machine, plus upload-specific states. Never fake a successful read; never hide a redundant or failed one.

| State | Meaning | Founder-facing honesty |
|---|---|---|
| **Uploading** | File transferring. | Progress. |
| **Reading** | Extracting content. | *"Reading your pitch deck… 14 slides…"* |
| **Synced** | Extracted, evidence stored, reflection ready. | Grounded reflection with document+location chips. |
| **Partial** | Some pages/sections read, some failed. | Reflection from what read; gaps stated. |
| **Empty** | Readable file, no meaningful content. | *"I opened it but couldn't find much to read."* No fabrication. |
| **Redundant** | Substantially duplicates known connected reality. | *"This looks like your website content, which I've already read — I'll keep anything new."* Honest, not accusatory. |
| **Unsupported** | Not a supported type (or scanned/image-only PDF). | *"I can't read this type yet — a PDF, Word doc, or text file works best."* |
| **Failed** | Corrupt, oversized, or parse error. | Honest error + retry. |

`Redundant`, `Empty`, and `Unsupported` are features — they are how upload stays honest when a file gives nothing new or nothing readable.

## 11. Risks

Attacking the capability, M2.1-style:

- **The unsolvable-classifier trap (highest conceptual risk).** If M2.2 is built to *detect reconstruction intent*, it fails (§0). Mitigation: the entire spec reframes to provenance-typing + redundancy — approve that reframe or the milestone is mis-scoped.
- **Extraction quality (highest execution risk).** PDF/DOCX extraction is messier than HTML — multi-column decks, tables, text-as-image, weird exports. Thin extraction → thin honest reflection → "it didn't get my deck." Mitigation: first-class extraction-quality measurement (§12), honest `empty`/`partial` states, and the same "measure don't estimate" discipline that caught M2.1's extraction issues.
- **Redundancy detection accuracy.** Too aggressive → rejects a genuine doc that merely echoes the website's themes (false `redundant`). Too lax → double-counts retyped content. Mitigation: redundancy must require *substantial* overlap (high threshold, measured), and a `redundant` classification suppresses only the overlapping fragments, never the whole file — genuinely-new content in a partly-redundant doc still lands.
- **Security / parser exploits (highest severity risk).** Real. Mitigation: §9 in full; non-negotiable, not deferrable.
- **Prompt injection via document content.** Novel to upload. Mitigation: document text is inert evidence, never instruction context; fail-closed resolution as defense-in-depth (§9).
- **Provenance dishonesty via weak artifacts.** A flat text file could tempt false-precise anchoring. Mitigation: weak-provenance conservatism (§5.4) — coarse anchors honestly, never invented location-specificity.
- **Scope creep across formats.** Every format is a maintenance liability. Mitigation: T1 = PDF/DOCX/text only; everything else deferred behind demand (§3).
- **Engine non-determinism (inherited).** Same run-to-run variance M2.1 found; measure distributions, not single runs (§12).

## 12. Success metrics

- **Extraction coverage** — % of a document's genuine content that becomes real fragments, across a representative set of real artifact types (decks, memos, guides). The M2.2 analogue of website block-coverage; measured, not assumed.
- **Provenance specificity** — % of rendered claims anchored to a specific location (page/slide/section) vs whole-document. Higher is better; weak-provenance artifacts honestly lower it.
- **Resolution hit-rate** — inferred claims resolving to real upload fragments, measured as distributions across multiple runs per artifact (engine non-determinism, per M2.1).
- **Redundancy accuracy** — on a labeled set (genuine docs vs retyped-website docs): true-redundant caught, genuine docs not false-flagged.
- **Honesty invariants (pass/fail, not graded):** zero dangling refs on live runs; zero upload-only `inferred` claims about external reality; every rendered line traceable; `visibility: private` set on all upload fragments; redundant content never double-counted.
- **Time-to-first-reflection** — Beat 1 within the fast window (the ~30s-to-first-grounded-reflection promise, synthesis behind — per the corrected M2.1 spec).
- **The human criterion** — a founder uploads a real business document and says *"it actually understood my deck."*

## 13. Acceptance criteria

M2.2 is complete when:
1. A founder uploads a real PDF, DOCX, or text artifact and receives a two-beat, fully-traceable reflection — Beat 1 fast, Beat 2 (fusing upload + any connected website) behind.
2. Every rendered claim traces to a specific upload fragment with a document+location anchor (or honestly-coarse anchor for weak-provenance artifacts). No untraceable claim renders.
3. Retyped-website content is detected `redundant`, honestly surfaced, and contributes no new evidence — the primary reconstruction vector neutralized without an intent-classifier.
4. No upload-only evidence produces an `inferred` claim about external reality (epistemic ceiling holds); `visibility: private` on all upload fragments.
5. Honest `empty`/`partial`/`redundant`/`unsupported`/`failed` states — no fabrication when a file gives nothing.
6. Security controls live: content-based type detection, size/complexity caps, no embedded-content execution, isolated parsing, document text never in instruction position, files encrypted and erasable.
7. The engine is byte-identical throughout (`992666a6…`/`6dff794d…`); the connector→evidence→engine→reflection boundary and the three-layer honesty gate are unchanged.
8. Extraction coverage, provenance specificity, redundancy accuracy, and hit-rate measured across a representative real-artifact set (distributions, not single runs) and reported.
9. Full suite green; upload connector implements the full standard contract; nothing in the website connector regressed.
10. **Human criterion met:** a founder uploads a real document and says it understood it.

## 14. Recommended implementation order

Capability-as-unit-of-delivery — but with **one internal review gate** at the single architectural-risk point, because if the classification model is wrong, everything above it is wasted (the CTO amendment from §0).

**Phase 1 — Foundation + the risk gate (build, then STOP for review):**
1. Upload connector skeleton (full contract, unauthenticated), file intake, content-based type detection, security bounds (§9 core: caps, isolation, no-execution).
2. PDF + DOCX + text extraction → structured content with location anchors.
3. Evidence generation: `observed-artifact` fragments, `source: upload`, document identity, anchors, `visibility: private`, through the unchanged honesty gate.
4. **Artifact classification (§4): provenance-typing + redundancy detection against existing `observed` fragments.**
   → **INTERNAL REVIEW GATE.** Before building recompute/reflection/UI on top: prove classification works — redundancy correctly catches retyped-website content, genuine docs aren't false-flagged, provenance strength is assigned correctly, measured on a labeled set. *This is the M2.2 equivalent of M2.1's honesty-spine checkpoint: validate the load-bearing thing before building the surface on it.* If classification is wrong, we fix it here, cheaply, before the rest exists.

**Phase 2 — Understanding (after gate passes):**
5. Recompute integration: upload evidence → shared frozen-engine path → fail-closed resolution spanning website+upload sources → two-beat reflection.
6. Epistemic-ceiling enforcement (no upload-only external-reality inferences).

**Phase 3 — Surface + honesty states:**
7. Upload UX (drag/drop, progress, document+location chips), the full honest state machine (§10), error handling.

**Phase 4 — Measure + close:**
8. Measurement across the representative real-artifact set (§12), distributions not single runs; honesty invariants verified live (dangling-refs=0, private-visibility, no external-reality upload inferences); full suite green; extraction quality assessed against real documents.
9. Report against acceptance criteria; then finishing pass (commit, spec correction if any, no engine touch).

The single gate at step 4 preserves the capability-as-unit model everywhere it's safe while keeping one measurement checkpoint at the exact place M2.2 could go architecturally wrong — the reconstruction/provenance problem you correctly identified as the core.

---

## Closing CTO assertion

The Upload Connector is the right next capability, and it's genuinely harder than the website in two specific ways that this spec refuses to hand-wave: **the reconstruction problem** (solved by reframing from unwinnable intent-detection to honest provenance-typing + redundancy) and **security** (a real file-handling attack surface that is in scope, not deferrable). Everything else reuses M2.1's proven machinery — the connector contract, the three-layer honesty gate, the shared recompute path, the frozen engine, the two-beat reflection, the fail-closed resolution.

The one thing to approve or reject before anything else: **the §0 reframing.** If we accept that we type provenance and detect redundancy rather than detect intent, M2.2 is buildable, honest, and complete as one capability. If we insist on detecting reconstruction intent from bytes, we should not build it as specified — because that classifier can't be built honestly, and pretending it can is the one thing that would let this capability violate ADR-007.
