# Evidence & Trust Model

How Business Brain represents what it knows, and how that earns the founder's trust.
This governs Act I and the surfaces beyond it. It is a presentation/semantics contract,
not an implementation spec.

## Principle Zero — earned kind, not silence

Business Brain is never silent about provenance, and never overstates it. Every statement
it makes carries an **origin** — the kind of knowing behind it. The goal is not to hedge
everything into vagueness, and not to hide uncertainty behind confident prose. It is to
say each thing in the *kind* it has actually earned. Honesty is the kindness; silence and
overclaim are both failures.

## The four origins

Every statement BB shows travels with one of four origin kinds (metadata that moves with
the data, surfaced only where it aids the founder's understanding — never flooded onto the UI):

| origin | meaning | voice |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `i-know` | a fact BB was given or established | stated plainly |
| `i-observed-sample` | read from evidence (in M1: a labeled **sample**, never the founder's own) | "here's how I'd read this" |
| `you-told-me` | the founder said it directly | quoted/attributed back to them |
| `i-suspect` | an inference or hypothesis, not yet confirmed | "my current hypothesis: …" |

`i-suspect` must *read* as a hypothesis. A reframe or deduction is phrased as one
("my current hypothesis: …"), never as established fact.

## The progression: I-observed → confirm → I-understand

An observation is not understanding. It becomes understanding only when the founder
confirms it. The trust ladder:

1. **I observed** (`i-observed-sample`) — BB surfaces a read of the evidence.
2. **Confirm** — the founder says "yes, that's right." The observation is **promoted** to
   understood; it may now carry forward as something BB understands.
3. **I understand** — only confirmed (or founder-stated) things are treated as understood.

### The "Not quite" branch — demotes, does not promote

If the founder says "Not quite", the original observation is **demoted**, never promoted.
It is not carried forward as understood. The correction the founder types becomes a new
`you-told-me` statement that **replaces** the observation. Only that corrected version
carries forward. BB must never treat a rejected read as if it were confirmed — a wrong
guess the founder corrected cannot reappear downstream as "what I understand about you."

## No unselected sources cited as observed

BB may only present evidence from sources that are actually in scope. It must never cite a
source the founder did not connect as something it "observed" about *them*. In the M1
preview this is enforced two ways at once:

- **(a)** the Seeing renders only the sources the founder selected in Connect; and
- **(b)** the entire Seeing is framed as a labeled **sample business**, so even the
  selected-source evidence reads as the sample's, never as a claim about the founder's
  own business (no real analysis exists yet).

## Provenance, not process

When BB explains *why* it asked something or *why* a plan follows, it names **where** the
conclusion came from ("Because you told me X…", "This follows from what you just
confirmed…") — never **how** it was computed. Provenance builds trust; exposed
chain-of-thought does not.

## Act I confidence language — single point

Act I speaks in single, plain statements, not confidence scores or probability bands. The
honesty lives in the origin kind and the confirm/correct ladder, not in numbers. No
percentages, no meters.

## UI restraint

Origin metadata is internal. Surface it only where it genuinely aids provenance — the
sample marker, the reasoning-bridge lines at scene seams, and distinguishing a hypothesis
(`i-suspect`) from an observation. The default presentation stays editorial and quiet.
